If you don't think games have been used as tools of seriousness to understand complexity, look no further than WWII and how young women reinvented strategic warfare using war-gaming.

In 1824, a Prussian lieutenant named Georg von Reisswitz presented a new war simulation to the Chief of the Prussian General Staff. The game used a scaled terrain map, contoured to represent actual topography. The playing pieces represented actual unit types with actual movement rates and actual combat ratios. Casualties were calculated by dice modified for cover and weapons type. Each side had imperfect information — a referee knew the full state of the board, but the players knew only what their units could actually see.

The Chief of Staff, Karl von Müffling, played it. Then he said: *this is not a game at all, this is a training for war. I shall recommend it to the whole army.*

But it was what Reisswitz himself said that I keep returning to. When asked whether the point was to win or lose, he replied: *the winning or losing, in the sense of a card or board game, does not come into it.*

Not winning or losing. The simulation is for learning. The stakes are real — the learning prepares you for decisions where people die — but the simulation's value is precisely that it is not the real thing, that you can fail inside it without consequence, that you can try the thing you would not dare try in the field and find out what happens.

The workplace eliminated simulation. That is a large part of why it cannot learn.

## Play Is Older Than Culture (Huizinga Said It First)

Johan Huizinga published [*Homo Ludens*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homo_Ludens) in 1938 — the same year Maslow visited the Siksika, the same year the world was heading toward the largest military application of game theory in human history. His argument: play is not an activity that human beings do in their leisure time. Play is a fundamental category of human existence, prior to culture, prior to civilization. Culture does not produce play. Play produces culture. Ritual, law, poetry, philosophy, art, warfare — Huizinga traces all of these to the play impulse and the magic circle, the bounded, rule-governed, consequence-limited space in which human beings practice the full range of their capacities.

*The play-mood is one of rapture and enthusiasm, and is sacred or festive in accordance with the occasion. A feeling of exaltation and tension accompanies the action.*

That sentence describes the state the DEAD game's performance management systems are designed to produce, but cannot. You can mandate attendance. You cannot mandate the play-mood. Huizinga knew that in 1938. Taylor missed it in 1911.

## For Over a Millenium, Chess and Go

Chess emerged in India as *chaturanga* around the sixth century CE, spread through Persia as *chatrang*, reached Europe as *shatranj* by the ninth century, and was formalized into its current rules in the fifteenth. It has been used to train strategic thinking — to simulate the experience of managing complex interdependent decisions under incomplete information with real consequences — for fifteen hundred years.

Go is older. Chinese records mention it from around 500 BCE. The game has a higher branching factor than chess — more possible positions than atoms in the observable universe — and has been used in East Asian military and administrative training for millennia. For over a millennium, games like Chess and Go have honed strategic thinking capabilities under the guise of warring states competing for domination. They inspired unique ways of thinking about new possibilities amid the dark sides of gaming.

I would even argue that rulers of past engaged in social game through Tarot and Astrology in order to seek new approaches and thinking about their dilemmas.

The Tarot de Marseille emerged in fifteenth-century Europe, probably from earlier Islamic and Byzantine game-card traditions. Whether you treat it as occult or not, what the Major Arcana offers is a map of the kinds of situations that recur in human experience, rendered in images that activate multiple interpretive frameworks simultaneously. You pull a card not to find out what will happen but to find out what question you are not asking. The situation you thought was about strategy might be about trust. The problem you thought was technical might be about power.

This is decision technology. It is the activation of different interpretive lenses on a situation you are too close to to see clearly. Tarot does not tell you what to do. It asks: have you looked at this from the other side?

The [wrong rulebook](/vgfw-blog/2026/03/04/vgfw-a3a-the-wrong-rulebook.html) has no category for this kind of tool. It cannot be certified, measured, or standardized. It produces insights that cannot be tracked in a dashboard. That is, precisely, why it has survived outside institutions for five hundred years while the institutions have cycled through paradigm after paradigm in search of the tool they keep building systems to preclude.

## The Women Who Played Wargames (The Part of the History That Gets Left Out)

During the Second World War, the British military trained women as wargame operators — running complex multi-player strategic simulations for military planning. Their reports consistently noted that they outperformed male counterparts on coordination tasks, information management, and multi-variable tracking. The military drew the wrong lesson: female operators were good at detail management, assisting the strategists.

The actual lesson was that the collaborative coordination work of complex simulation favored skill sets that training had consistently developed in women and actively discouraged in men.

I think about this every time someone frames gaming as a male domain. The history of game-based learning is not an unbroken male line from Kriegsspiel to Dungeons & Dragons to video games. It has always included the people who were using simulation as a tool for developing exactly the capacities the formal system said they could not have.

## D&D Put the Player at the Center

Gary Gygax and Dave Arneson published the first edition of [Dungeons & Dragons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons) in 1974 by doing something previous wargames hadn't: putting the player at the center of the simulation. Previous wargames simulated armies or units. D&D simulated the individual — the choices, the development, the accumulated experience of a single person navigating a complex world over time.

The mechanics that emerged: character sheets as visible capability maps, XP as explicit progress tracking, level advancement as earned rather than ascribed, skills developed through use rather than through credentials, the dungeon master as the host who creates conditions for player agency rather than the authority who determines outcomes.

Fifty years later, the raid leader in a World of Warcraft guild is doing something recognizable to anyone who has run an Art of Hosting event or facilitated a Theory U process. Sixty people, coordinating in real time, each with different skills and roles, achieving something none of them could accomplish alone, under a leader whose authority is entirely earned through demonstrated competence and whose role is to create conditions for others to do their best work.

The guild leader cannot mandate the raid. They can only make the conditions good enough that people choose to show up. That is leadership. The MBA does not teach it. The game does.

## What Gaming Adds to This Tradition

All of this precedes video games. I am not arguing that video games are uniquely important. I am arguing that video games have, in the past forty years, developed the largest and most thoroughly playtested laboratory for human motivation, cooperation, skill development, and meaning-making in the history of the species — and that the field of organizational design has largely failed to notice.

The number of person-hours invested in understanding what makes a game compelling — what creates genuine engagement, voluntary effort, sustained attention, intrinsic motivation, the desire to keep playing even after the extrinsic rewards run out — vastly exceeds the equivalent investment in understanding what makes work compelling. And the findings are not mysterious. They converge on the same principles from multiple directions: clear progress feedback, visible skill trees, meaningful choices, cooperation that is genuinely cooperative, tolerance for failure as a learning mechanism, the possibility of mastery, stakes that are real without being fatal.

Kriegsspiel in 1824. D&D in 1974. World of Warcraft in 2004. Hades in 2020. Each iteration adds fidelity, scale, and accessibility to what Reisswitz was building. The simulation does not replace the real. It trains you for it. It lets you practice dying and coming back. It lets you fail where the cost of failure is a restart rather than a ruin.

The workplace eliminated simulation. It does not have to.

What are we building next?
