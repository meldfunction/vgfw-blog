# VGFW | There Are Two Doors
### An Introduction to *Video Games For Work*

**Video Games For Work** is a series about play, power, and the redesign of systems that shape human life. This is the Opener.

[Door A: The DEAD Game →](./01-A1-the-dead-game.md) | [Door B: The Pyramid Was Always Upside Down →](./06-B1-pyramid-upside-down.md)

---

At some point — maybe recently, maybe years ago, maybe this morning — you looked up from what you were doing and thought: this is not the game I meant to be playing.

Maybe you were in a meeting scheduled to discuss the outcomes of a previous meeting. Maybe you were updating a spreadsheet that feeds a dashboard that informs a report that gets presented to people who will use it to justify a decision that's already been made. Maybe you were doing everything right — hitting the targets, climbing the ladder, collecting the credentials — and you arrived at a destination that felt, somehow, like the wrong country. Flags you didn't recognize. Language you'd learned but didn't love.

Or maybe you never bought in. Maybe you always knew the game was off but couldn't find the words, couldn't find the people sensing the same thing, couldn't find anything that named the specific texture of the wrongness without turning it into a self-help promise or a political manifesto.

This series is neither.

It is an attempt to name the game — the actual game, the one running for centuries underneath the org charts and performance reviews and quarterly targets and rhetoric about disruption — and then ask the harder question: what game do we actually want to play?

---

Here is what we know, as of February 2026.

Seventy million white-collar workers in the United States are sitting at desks that a CEO somewhere has already decided are too expensive to keep. Not because the work is bad. Because it can now be done by something that doesn't need health insurance or a salary or a reason to get up in the morning. [Matt Shumer](https://shumer.dev/something-big-is-happening), who builds AI systems for a living, described February 5th of this year as the moment the water reached his chest. He sat down, described what he wanted built, walked away for four hours, came back to find it finished. Done well. Better than he would have done it himself.

He is not the last person you would expect this to happen to. He is the first.

[Andrew Yang calls what's coming the Fuckening.](https://blog.andrewyang.com/p/the-end-of-the-office) That feels about right.

And yet.

The question this series is asking is not how we survive the Fuckening. That is a real and urgent question and people like Yang are working on the policy answers — floors, safety nets, income guarantees — and those answers matter. But that is not the question here.

The question here is older and stranger: what were we doing before, and was it actually worth doing?

Because here is the thing about a burning building. It clarifies. When the thing you organized your entire life around starts to dissolve — when the job that was supposed to be the point turns out to be automatable, when the ladder loses its rungs, when the credential stops opening the door — you get, if you're paying attention, a rare and uncomfortable gift. You get to ask what you were climbing toward in the first place.

Most people don't ask. They look for another ladder.

This series is for the people who ask.

---

There is a book — thin, strange, beloved by an unlikely coalition — called *Jonathan Livingston Seagull.* Richard Bach published it in 1970 after eighteen rejections. Nine million copies sold.

A seagull who refuses to fish. Who is interested, instead, in flight itself. The flock finds this baffling, then threatening, then unforgivable. They cast him out. He keeps flying.

Strip away the allegory — the Christian undertones, the California mysticism — and what you have is a precise description of a very specific problem: what do you do when you've mastered the game your community was playing and found it insufficient?

Jonathan is not anti-ambition. He is the most ambitious bird in the book. He is anti-fishing. Anti the assumption that flying exists in service of catching something, that the skill is justified only by its yield, that the point of mastery is the reward at the end of it.

The flock eats. Jonathan flies. The flock thinks flying is how you get fish. Jonathan knows flying is the point.

What this series keeps returning to is the part of the story that gets less attention. Jonathan goes back. After the mastery, after the exile, after discovering what flight actually is — he returns to the flock. He teaches. The point of mastery was never the mastery. It was what you do with it afterward. What you give back. What you make possible for the next bird who was always capable of more than fishing and never had anyone show them how.

Every serious tradition has a word for this move. The bodhisattva who achieves enlightenment and refuses the final exit, turning back to serve the liberation of everyone still in the water. The guru whose lineage exists not to glorify the teacher but to make the teacher unnecessary. Plato building the Academy not to house his own thinking but to train the next generation to think better than he did. Same move. Different costume. Every tradition found it.

The pattern is everywhere. This series is built on it.

Not: how do you win.
Not: how do you survive.
But: what do you do when you find out what you're actually flying for?

## There are two ways in. Both lead to the same place.

**[Door A: The Diagnosis](./01-A1-the-dead-game.md)** is for people who need to understand what broke before imagining what might replace it. It begins with the game that's actually been running — what we're calling the DEAD game — and moves through the digital feudalism of the platform economy, the Taylorist assumptions baked into every job description you've ever read, the learning systems never designed to produce the kind of people they claimed to be producing, and the soul work that got stripped out when work became just a job. Structural. Diagnostic. Occasionally enraging. Names things. Cites sources. Does not console.

**[Door B: The Imagination](./06-B1-pyramid-upside-down.md)** is for people who need to imagine before they can diagnose. It begins with a pyramid that was built upside down — a theft so quiet and so long ago that we mistook the inversion for the original — and moves through the history of games as civilization technology, from Prussian war rooms to tarot cards to forty years of role-playing games. It asks what gaming figured out about human motivation that four hundred years of management theory missed. Philosophical. Historical. Occasionally electric.

Both doors end at the same essay: a synthesis that argues the new game is not coming. It is already running. In the rage-quits and lateral moves and trusted small teams built inside broken large organizations to get the real work done. In the side projects and skills developed outside job descriptions and communities of practice that formed because the institution couldn't hold what people actually needed.

You are already playing a different game. This series tries to help you see it clearly enough to play it on purpose.

Start at Door A if the diagnosis needs to come before the vision. Finish at the synthesis, then walk back through Door B.

Start at Door B if the imagination comes easier than the autopsy. Same instruction in reverse.

Or start wherever the title pulls you. These essays cross-reference each other. You can find your way.
