The game board has always been geography.

Before platforms. Before global supply chains. Before the corporation that employs you is headquartered in a state you have never visited and owned by investors you will never meet. Before all of that, the unit of economic life was the place where you lived — the watershed, the valley, the neighborhood, the town — and the people you could actually see and know and hold accountable because they had to look you in the eye at the market on Saturday.

What the [technofeudalism](/vgfw-blog/2026/03/03/vgfw-a2-rise-of-technofeudalism.html) essay describes — the colonization of local economies by platform infrastructure that the local community neither owns nor governs — also demonstrated something the platforms didn't intend to: that people still want what local economies provided. Farmers markets have grown for twenty consecutive years. Local food co-ops are expanding. The craft brewing industry in the United States went from 89 breweries in 1980 to over 9,000 in 2023. People are willing to pay more, inconvenience themselves more, for something with a face behind it.

That is not nostalgia. That is demand for a different game board.

## Three Experiments Running Now

**Transition Towns.** In 2006, Rob Hopkins founded the [Transition Town](https://transitionnetwork.org) movement in Totnes, England, with a question: what would our community look like if we designed it for resilience rather than efficiency? What if we mapped our local food production, energy generation, skills exchange, and care networks, and then deliberately built toward sufficiency rather than dependency?

The Transition Network now operates in over fifty countries, with thousands of local initiatives mapping and strengthening the local capacity their communities have been allowing to atrophy for decades. Some of these are visible and formal — the community energy cooperative, the local currency, the repair café. Many are less visible: the skill-sharing networks, the seed libraries, the mutual aid relationships that activate when the supply chain fails and the local network is the only thing left.

This is not anti-growth. It is pro-resilience. The game board is the bioregion. The design principle: what can we produce here, what do we need from outside, and how do we reduce the second category until it consists only of things genuinely unavailable locally.

**Mondragon at City Scale.** The [Mondragon Corporation](https://www.mondragon-corporation.com) in the Basque Country is the most thoroughly documented large-scale cooperative experiment in the twentieth century — 80,000 worker-owners, federated across manufacturing, retail, finance, and education, with a pay ratio between highest and lowest earners that no publicly traded company has approached. It began with a technical school and fifteen workers in 1941.

What is less often discussed is the urban and bioregional dimension of Mondragon's design. The cooperatives are embedded in the Basque economy in ways that keep value local. The cooperative bank [Caja Laboral](https://www.cajalaboral.com) was a deliberate strategy for keeping capital in the region rather than having it extracted by distant investors. The technical schools produced workers with the specific skills the regional economy needed, rather than general credentials for a national labor market.

The model is not directly transferable. The Basque Country's history, culture, and political context are specific. But the design logic is transferable. Someone has to adapt it.

**Civic Tech and the Open City.** [Code for America](https://www.codeforamerica.org) — and its international equivalents in the Code for All network — began from a specific observation: cities hold enormous amounts of data about the lives of their residents, residents could use that data to understand and navigate their relationship to city services, and the technology to make that possible was available but not deployed because the institutional capacity to deploy it was missing.

What emerged from a decade of Code for America brigades was less a set of specific applications and more a practice: technologists and community organizers and city government staff working together to understand what residents actually need, build the smallest possible thing that serves that need, test it with real people, and iterate. That practice — which looks a lot like [Theory U's](/vgfw-blog/2026/03/05/vgfw-a3b-the-missing-curriculum.html) sensing journey and rapid prototyping — is transferable to any city willing to treat its residents as co-designers rather than service recipients.

## PLATFORMS RULE EVERYTHING… OR DO THEY?

[CoopCycle](https://coopcycle.org), a federation of bicycle delivery cooperatives across Europe, has built open-source logistics software that any city's delivery cooperative can use — a direct challenge to the platform architecture of Deliveroo and Uber Eats, where the platform extracts rent and the riders bear all the risk. The software is the commons. The cooperatives are the local operators. The city is the game board.

This is what I mean when I say the alternative is already running. It is not waiting for permission. It is also not fully scaled. Both things are true.

## The Geography Question (Work in Progress)

Here is the question I cannot fully answer: what does it mean to organize economic life around geography in a world where the most valuable work is increasingly digital and location-independent?

The pandemic proved that knowledge work can happen from anywhere. It also proved that anywhere quickly becomes somewhere specific — the home, the neighborhood, the city — and that the quality of that somewhere matters enormously for the quality of the work and the life. Remote work did not abolish geography. It redistributed it.

That redistribution is an opening. The places that receive knowledge workers who want something different — who are already playing a different game and need a game board that supports it — have an unusual opportunity to design for that population rather than compete for the headquarters of companies that will never arrive.

What is your game board? What does it need to be able to produce?

I am genuinely curious.
