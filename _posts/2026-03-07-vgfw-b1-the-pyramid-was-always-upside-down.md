I was thinking recently that Maslow would be rolling in his grave, if he heard how the hierarchy of needs became weaponized as a pyramid — a.k.a. the tip of the spear of individual progress over collective progress.

In the summer of 1938, Abraham Maslow traveled to Alberta, Canada, to spend time with the Siksika Nation — a Blackfoot people — at the invitation of anthropologist Jane Richardson. He was a young psychologist, not yet famous, trying to understand human motivation. He stayed for about six weeks.

What he encountered, by his own notes, was a society organized around a fundamentally different theory of human flourishing than anything in the Western psychological tradition. He wrote about it. He incorporated aspects of what he saw into his thinking. He built a pyramid and put self-actualization at the top of it and published it in 1943.

The Blackfoot tipi has community actualization at the base. Self-actualization in the middle. Cultural perpetuity at the apex. The direction of the pyramid is inverted.

This is not a small difference. It is the whole argument.

---

## What Maslow Inverted (And Why It Matters Now)

The Western psychological tradition Maslow was working in had a specific problem it was trying to solve: how do you motivate the individual? The individual is the unit. The goal is individual flourishing. The hierarchy is a ladder the individual climbs. The apex — self-actualization — is the prize you get for having your lower needs met.

The Blackfoot framework has a different unit and a different apex. The base — the widest, most fundamental layer — is community actualization. The community's capacity to provide for the flourishing of its members is the foundation on which individual development rests, not the reward that follows from it. Self-actualization sits in the middle. It is important. It is not the point. The point — the apex — is cultural perpetuity: the survival and flourishing of the community across generations, the transmission of what makes this particular way of being human possible for the people who come after.

Jonathan Livingston Seagull's return makes sense from a Blackfoot tipi. From Maslow's pyramid, it's an anomaly. Why would the fully self-actualized seagull go back? He's at the top. There is nothing above the top.

From the tipi, it is obvious. The development of the individual's full capacity is in service of what the community needs to carry forward. Mastery is not the end. It is the means by which the culture perpetuates itself.

[Leanne Betasamosake Simpson](https://www.leannesimpson.ca) puts the problem plainly: the extraction of Indigenous knowledge by Western academia is not neutral. It is part of the same colonial project as the extraction of land and resources. Maslow took what he encountered in Alberta, inverted its fundamental structure, removed its communal and ecological embeddedness, and produced a pyramid that the corporation could use to design individual incentive systems. The knowledge was extracted. The source was not cited. The inversion was not acknowledged.

I think about this every time I see the Maslow pyramid in a presentation deck.

## Who Holds the Pen

The pyramid-building problem is structural, not personal. When a framework for understanding human motivation is built by a single researcher from a single cultural tradition, drawing on observations of another cultural tradition's knowledge, the framework will encode the assumptions of the builder. Not out of malice. Out of the shape of the lens.

[Design Justice](https://designjustice.org), the framework developed by Sasha Costanza-Chock and the Design Justice Network, asks a foundational question about any design process: who is in the room when the design happens? The design of a system encodes the assumptions, values, and priorities of the people who built it. Those assumptions become invisible — they feel like common sense rather than choices — until you examine who they leave out.

Whose knowledge was not in the room when the management pyramid was built? Women, whose relational and care labor has never fit the individualist model. Workers of color, whose collective organizing and mutual aid traditions were systematically suppressed while their labor built the industrial base. Indigenous peoples, whose sophisticated frameworks for governing commons and transmitting knowledge across generations were treated as primitive rather than sophisticated alternatives. Workers in the Global South. People with disabilities.

[Equity-Centered Community Design](https://www.creativereactionlab.com/our-approach) from Creative Reaction Lab takes this further: design justice is not just about adding diverse voices to existing processes. It is about shifting power — who sets the agenda, who decides what counts as a problem, who evaluates the solutions. The curb cut effect demonstrates that designing for the most constrained user produces better outcomes for everyone. But producing it required someone with a wheelchair at the design table. The insight was obvious once the right person was in the room.

You may wonder who would be in the room if we were designing the management pyramid today, from scratch, with a genuinely different guest list…

## What Automation Is Actually Doing (The Uncomfortable Good News)

Here is the uncomfortable version of the good news.

If AI systems automate the two bottom tiers of the Maslow pyramid — if physiological and safety needs can be provisioned with less labor, which is the direction this is going even if the transition is uneven and brutal and full of friction — then the remaining question for human beings is not survival. It is meaning.

This is the Blackfoot tipi forced on civilization. Not because anyone chose it. Because the logical endpoint of automating everything that can be automated is a world in which the only remaining question is what the community is for and what we are going to transmit to the people who come after.

Andrew Yang's UBI addresses the survival tier. The [Institute for the Future's Universal Basic Assets](https://www.iftf.org/projects/uba/) addresses the ownership tier. [Open Source Ecology's](https://www.opensourceecology.org) Global Village Construction Set addresses the physical production tier. Each is a floor, a foundation. None of them, on their own, addresses the question the Blackfoot tipi puts at the apex: what is the community for, and what does it need to perpetuate across time?

That question has no policy answer. It has only a practice answer.

---

## Minecraft Is the Tipi in Pixels

*Minecraft*, which has sold more copies than any video game in history, has no win condition in its default creative mode. You are given infinite resources and an infinite world. You can build anything. Nothing is lost if you build it wrong. Nothing is won if you build it right.

What players build, consistently, across cultures and age groups and languages, are things for each other. Elaborate collaborative worlds. Functioning cities. Recreations of real buildings. Environments designed not for the builder's satisfaction but for others to inhabit and explore. The creative mode is, in practice, a community perpetuity engine. The individual's capacity is exercised in service of something that others will live in.

This is tipi logic in pixels. Nobody designed the player behavior. It emerged when you gave people infinite creative resources, removed survival pressure, and offered connection with others doing the same. What humans naturally do with full creative freedom, no threat, and community — they build things together.

The pyramid said self-actualization was the ceiling. The tipi said it was the floor. Minecraft ran the experiment. Forty million people playing cooperatively with no win condition and no end, for a game running since 2009, is a data point about human motivation that no management textbook has adequately integrated.

I wonder when they will.
