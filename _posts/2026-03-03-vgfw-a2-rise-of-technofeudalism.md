There is a sentence in Yanis Varoufakis's 2023 book *Technofeudalism: What Killed Capitalism* that I have not been able to put down since reading it. He is describing the relationship between a small business and Amazon Marketplace, between a musician and Spotify, between a driver and Uber. The sentence: *the cloudalists don't need to deploy bailiffs to confiscate or evict. Every vassal knows that with the removal of a link from their platform they could lose access to the bulk of their customers. A sanitized tech-terror is the bedrock of technofeudalism.*

Sanitized tech-terror.

Hold that phrase for a moment. Then think about the last time you checked your phone.

---

## PLATFORMS RULE EVERYTHING AROUND ME, DOLLA DOLLA BILL Y'ALL

For centuries, the dominant form of wealth extraction was rent. You owned land. Others farmed it. You collected a portion of whatever they produced. The farmer had no alternative because land was finite, you owned it, and survival required access to it.

Then capitalism happened. Industrialization created profit extracted from wage labor, and rent became secondary. Markets emerged. Competition, however imperfect, created at least the theoretical possibility of alternatives. You could leave one employer and find another. You could, in principle, start your own business.

I believe this era is over. What replaced capitalism's profit-driven logic is a new form of rent — cloud rent — extracted not from physical land but from digital territory. Amazon is not a market. It is a fiefdom. Sellers do not compete on Amazon. They pay rent to Amazon for access to customers they could not otherwise reach, and Amazon's algorithm-sheriff determines who prospers and who disappears. The same logic governs Spotify, Uber, Instagram, the App Store, Google Search, and every other platform now sitting between producers and the people who want what they produce.

With cloud capital, much of its stock is being produced not by employees but by users at large — unpaid people who come into contact with its algorithms and work for free to imbue them with a greater capacity to attract other users. You are not the customer. You are not even the product. You are the serf who builds the lord's castle while living in the field outside it.

Call it technofeudalism or late capitalism or platform capitalism. It is the [DEAD game](/vgfw-blog/2026/03/02/vgfw-a1-the-dead-game/) wearing a hoodie.

## The Promise Was Different (And They Knew It)

I want to remember the promise, because amnesia about the promise is part of how the extraction stays normalized.

The early internet — before the enclosure, before the platforms — was something genuinely strange and new. Ungoverned, anarchic, deeply weird, and alive with the possibility that networks of humans could organize themselves without the permission of institutions. People built things and gave them away. Knowledge moved freely. The distance between an idea and its audience collapsed.

Then came the privatization. The platform companies arrived and did something elegant and devastating: they took the ungoverned commons and turned it into a shopping mall. Free to enter. Private ownership. Your behavior tracked, sold, and used to build more sophisticated traps. The home page gave way to the feed. The forum gave way to the group. The link gave way to the walled garden. And somewhere in that transition, the internet stopped being a technology that empowered people and became a technology that empowered its owners.

Elon Musk buying Twitter is the clearest recent illustration. Not for the advertising revenue. For the territory. For the chokepoint. The lord does not farm the land. The lord owns access to it. That is sufficient.

## The "Sharing Economy" (The Most Audacious Branding in Labor History)

The gig economy was sold as liberation.

Be your own boss. Set your own hours. Monetize your skills. The *sharing economy*, they called it. I have to stop and appreciate that. Nothing was being shared. What was being shared was the risk, transferred from the corporation to the worker, while the reward flowed in the other direction.

Trebor Scholz at The New School has spent years documenting what the gig economy actually did: it spread market relations deeper into human life, created new forms of hyper-exploitation, and stripped workers of protections they previously held as employees — minimum wage, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation, the right to organize. An Uber driver is not liberated from the boss. The boss has been replaced by an algorithm that cannot be reasoned with, negotiated with, or appealed to. The algorithm does not have bad days. It does not have mercy. It optimizes.

[Deloitte's 2025 survey](https://www.deloitte.com/global/en/issues/work/content/genzmillennialsurvey.html) found forty-eight percent of Gen Z workers reporting financial insecurity, up from thirty percent the year before. The platform economy promised a new kind of freedom. It delivered a new kind of precarity with better UX.

And yet — the platforms are genuinely useful. I probably used one today. You did too. The convenience is real. The DEAD game is very good at producing things people actually want. That is part of what makes it difficult to exit and easy to criticize from the outside while using it constantly. I am not exempt from this. Neither are you. I think it's worth saying that plainly.

But holding both things at once does not mean pretending the extraction isn't happening. The serf built the castle. The castle is genuinely beautiful. The serf still lives in the field.

## The Alternative Is Already Running

Trebor Scholz's answer to all this is platform cooperativism — the idea that the technology of platforms is not inherently feudal, only that its current ownership structure is. There is nothing technically inevitable about Uber's design that requires its profits to flow to investors rather than drivers. The code is not the problem. The ownership is.

This is not a utopian fantasy. It exists.

[Stocksy United](https://www.stocksy.com) is a photographer-owned stock photography cooperative. Its 1,800 artist-members own the platform, share in its revenue, and govern its decisions. [Drivers Cooperative](https://drivers.coop) in New York City is a ride-hailing cooperative with over 9,000 member-drivers who own and govern the platform they work on. [Smart.coop](https://smart.coop) in Europe has 35,000 members across multiple countries. [Mondragon](https://www.mondragon-corporation.com) in the Basque Country has been running a federation of worker-owned cooperatives since 1956 — 80,000 worker-owners across finance, manufacturing, retail, and education, with a pay ratio between highest and lowest earners that no publicly traded company approaches.

Mondragon started in 1941 with a technical college run by a Jesuit priest in a small Basque town. Fifteen years before the first cooperatives opened. Scale is not given. It is built. And it is built, historically, by people who started before the conditions were favorable.

The [Institute for the Future's Universal Basic Assets framework](https://www.iftf.org/projects/uba/) adds a complementary angle. Beyond cash, beyond UBI's floor, UBA argues for access to three classes of assets: private assets like housing and financial security, public assets like healthcare and education, and open assets — Wikipedia, open-source software, scientific knowledge, and increasingly AI tools — that are communally created and available to all. The open asset category is where platform cooperativism and UBA converge: the value of the internet was created by everyone who ever used it. The question of who owns the infrastructure that captures that value is political, not technical.

## What the DEAD Game Cannot Do in Digital Form

It cannot manufacture genuine trust.

It can simulate trust — with ratings systems and reviews and the theater of community that platforms perform — but it cannot create the thing itself. Trust is built through repeated interaction between people who have something at stake together. The algorithm can optimize for its proxies. It cannot produce the substance.

Genuine trust is load-bearing. It is what makes cooperation possible, what makes governance of shared resources possible, what makes any system more complex than a transaction hold together over time. The guild — which I will return to in the synthesis — is built on trust. Not the platform's simulated version. The real kind. The kind that takes time, requires vulnerability, and cannot be extracted by an algorithm because it is not a resource.

It is a relationship.

That is where the next game begins. Not in the destruction of the platforms but in the construction of something beside them that people would rather belong to. Some of that construction is already happening. More in [The Local Game](/vgfw-blog/2026/03/12/vgfw-the-local-game/).

---

**Game note:** EVE Online, again. Players have built *Citadels* — player-owned space stations that function as genuine alternatives to NPC station infrastructure. They run their own markets, set their own taxes, govern their own access rules. The game designers didn't anticipate the extent of it. When you give people the tools to build cooperative alternatives inside a competitive system, some of them will. They need the tools and the incentive. Right now, outside the game, we have increasingly sophisticated tools and a collapsing incentive to stay in the existing structure. That combination has historically produced something new.
