*If you came through Door B first, welcome to the other side.*

Let me start with a word you already know and a meaning you were never supposed to find.

The word is *management.* You have been managed. You have perhaps managed others. You have sat in rooms where management was discussed as though it were a neutral technology — a set of tools for coordinating human effort toward shared goals. Efficient. Rational. Necessary.

Here is what management actually is: the application of industrial factory logic to human beings.

Not as metaphor. Literally. The management practices that govern most organizations today — the job description, the performance review, the org chart, the salary band, the quarterly target — were designed in the early twentieth century for factories. Frederick Winslow Taylor published [*The Principles of Scientific Management*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Principles_of_Scientific_Management) in 1911. Henry Ford built the assembly line. The logic was explicit: break work into its smallest components, remove worker discretion, measure output precisely, pay for compliance. The worker is an input. The product is the output. The manager is the interface between them.

We never stopped. We just changed the vocabulary.

## Dominate. Exploit. Adapt. Destroy. (In That Order, Every Time)

The framework underneath the modern corporate system has four moves. I call it DEAD because that is, precisely, what it does to the people inside it and eventually to the systems that run it.

**Dominate.** Establish control of the market, the narrative, the talent pool, the regulatory environment. Dominance is not a side effect of corporate success. It is the primary objective. Every major strategic decision in a publicly traded company is evaluated against its contribution to dominance. The world's first two-trillion-dollar company did not get there by accident. It got there by treating dominance as a design principle.

**Exploit.** Extract maximum value from every resource in the system. Labor. Land. Attention. Regulation. We say *leverage* now, or *optimize*, but the mechanism is identical. Find a source of value, extract from it at the maximum rate the system will tolerate, externalize the cost onto someone or something that cannot effectively push back. Workers who cannot organize. Communities that cannot relocate. Ecosystems that cannot invoice.

**Adapt.** When the extraction becomes visible — when the regulation arrives, when the press turns, when the workforce revolts — pivot. Rebrand. Acquire the critic. Publish a values statement. The adaptation is not a change of direction. It is a change of costume. Gordon Gecko said greed is good in 1987. Thirty years later Oliver Stone reflected that the scene had functioned as aspiration rather than warning for an entire generation of finance professionals. The costume changed. The game didn't.

**Destroy.** Obsolete the workforce before the workforce obsoletes you. This is where we are in February 2026, watching it happen in real time. [Andrew Yang, writing two weeks ago:](https://blog.andrewyang.com/p/the-end-of-the-office) *"I spoke to the CEO of a publicly traded tech company. He said, 'We're firing 15% of workers right now. We'll probably do another 20% two years from now. And then another 20% two years later.'"*

That is not disruption. That is the fourth move. It has always ended here.

DEAD is not a conspiracy. It is a logic. It does not require malice to operate — only that the incentive structure rewards each of its four moves, which it does, reliably, because the incentive structure was designed by the people who benefit from those moves. [Venkatesh Rao traced this logic back to the East India Company in 1600,](https://www.ribbonfarm.com/2011/06/08/a-brief-history-of-the-corporation-1600-to-2100/) through the Dutch trading monopolies, through the first industrial manufacturers, through the robber barons, to here. Same game. The technology changes. The four moves don't.

## The Fuckening, Actually

[Matt Shumer, who has spent six years building AI systems,](https://shumer.dev/something-big-is-happening) describes what the Destroy move looks like from the inside. On February 5th, 2026, OpenAI and Anthropic released new models on the same day. His description: *"I am no longer needed for the actual technical work of my job."*

He is a founder and investor in the AI space. He builds these tools. I find it darkly instructive that the last person you would expect to say this is the first person to say it publicly.

METR tracks this with data. They measure the length of real-world tasks — measured by how long they take a human expert — that an AI model can complete end-to-end without assistance. A year ago: roughly ten minutes. Then an hour. Then several hours. That number has been doubling approximately every seven months, with recent data suggesting acceleration to every four months.

Andrew Yang calls what's coming the Fuckening. That feels about right.

I want to hold something alongside the urgency though. Some researchers argue AI adoption will face significant friction, regulatory pushback, the natural limits of complex deployment in messy human organizations. The speed of what's coming is genuinely debated. What is not debated is the direction. And what the DEAD framework helps you see is that this is not a technological accident — the Destroy move was always coming. AI is not a disruption of the corporate project. It is its completion.

## Bullshit, Technically Speaking

David Graeber spent the last years of his life documenting something workers already knew: that a significant proportion of jobs created in the late twentieth and early twenty-first century consisted primarily of maintaining the appearance of activity rather than producing anything of genuine value. He called them [bullshit jobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bullshit_Jobs). Box-tickers. Duct-tapers. Flunkies. Goons. The roles that exist because hierarchy requires supervision, supervision requires administrators, administration requires coordinators, and coordination requires the kind of meetings that get scheduled to discuss the outcomes of other meetings.

His finding — drawn from surveys and testimonies of workers across industries and countries — was that a significant plurality of employed people believed their own jobs to be bullshit. Not difficult. Not unfulfilling. *Bullshit.* Producing nothing of genuine worth for anyone, in ways the people doing the jobs could recognize clearly and discuss with considerable articulacy.

AI will automate many of these roles. That is not, on its face, a loss. What it reveals is the question that was always lurking underneath: if the work was not producing genuine value, what were the people doing it for?

The answer, for most of them, was: a wage. And beneath the wage, a structure. A place to be. A set of expectations to meet. An identity. A community, however thin. The job was not just economic. It was, for many people, the primary organizing structure of their adult lives.

When the job goes, that structure goes with it. Nobody in the boardroom running the Destroy move has adequately answered for that.

## The Long Trail of Tears (The Part the Textbooks Skip)

Before I name what comes next, I want to be honest about where the DEAD game came from — not just from Taylor and Ford, but from a longer trail.

[Robin D.G. Kelley and the scholars of racial capitalism](https://harvardlawreview.org/2013/06/racial-capitalism/) have documented what the business school textbooks consistently bracket out: the accumulation of capital that funded the industrial revolution, and the management practices that organized it, were built on a foundation of enslaved and colonial labor. The corporation as a legal structure was developed by trading companies whose primary business was human trafficking and resource extraction from colonized territories. The DEAD game did not begin in a factory in Detroit in 1913. It began on plantations and in trading posts and in the legal instruments that made owning another human being a balance sheet asset.

This is not ancient history. Racial capitalism names the ongoing structure, not just the origin. The workers most exposed to the current displacement wave are not randomly distributed. The entry-level roles being eliminated fastest — customer service, data processing, administrative support — are disproportionately held by workers of color, by women, by workers in the Global South providing labor to Western companies at arbitrage rates that the automation wave will undercut entirely.

The DEAD game always had a hierarchy of who gets destroyed first. I think about that a lot.

[Deloitte's 2025 survey of 23,482 workers across 44 countries](https://www.deloitte.com/global/en/issues/work/content/genzmillennialsurvey.html) found Gen Z's average tenure in the first five years of their career is 1.1 years. Down from 1.8 for millennials, 2.8 for Gen X. The Great Resignation was not a tantrum. It was a data point. These are not apathetic people. They are people who correctly identified that the game they were invited to play does not deliver what it promised, and who are, in increasing numbers, refusing to pretend otherwise.

You may wonder what happens when the Destroy move lands on a generation that already withdrew its consent…

Courtney Devon, commenting on Yang's piece the day it dropped, asked the question the boardrooms haven't answered: *"Will unemployed people surviving on growing their own vegetables be buying $1500 smartphones? Will all these unemployed people be paying for Netflix?"* The consumer demand collapse built into mass displacement is not something the stock market currently prices in. It will be.

That is where the DEAD game ends. Not with a collapse. With an opening.

The next essay asks what's been colonizing that opening from the other side — the platform economy that arrived with promises of liberation and delivered a more sophisticated version of the same four moves.

**Game note:** EVE Online — the massively multiplayer space game running since 2003 — runs a fully player-driven economy. Give humans a sandbox and unrestricted economic freedom, and within months they recreate a DEAD-structured market: dominant player corporations monopolizing trade routes, exploiting smaller corps, adapting their branding, destroying competition through coordinated market manipulation. No developer scripted this. The players arrived at it themselves. The DEAD game is not imposed from outside. It emerges from the incentive structure. Change the incentive structure and you change the game. That's the whole bet.
