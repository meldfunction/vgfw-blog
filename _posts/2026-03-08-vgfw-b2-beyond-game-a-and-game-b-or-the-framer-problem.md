Game B is a thing now. There are conferences. There is a wiki. There are people who describe themselves as Game B thinkers in their bios and mean it sincerely.

The diagnosis is correct. The existing system — Game A — is producing catastrophic outcomes that cannot be solved by playing Game A better. Domination games. Win-lose structures. Short time horizons. The extraction of value from commons until the commons collapse. All of this is accurate and real.

The problem is the solution.

---

## The Framer Problem (And I Include Myself)

Daniel Schmachtenberger is sincere. Jim Rutt is sincere. The people who showed up to think seriously about what comes after Game A — what a civilization that has internalized complexity, long-term thinking, and non-rivalrous value creation might look like — are sincere.

They are also mostly people who were good at Game A.

This is not an attack. It is a structural observation. The people with the platform, the time, the vocabulary, and the network to design the next game are largely people who succeeded in the last one. Their disillusionment with Game A is real. Their access to Game B conversations was purchased with Game A credentials. The questions they find most interesting are the questions that occur to people who arrived at the frontier of Game A and found it insufficient.

Not everyone arrived at that frontier.

I look past the trail of tears present in Game A and Game B and posit: we need a new way to think about our individual and collective roles in society. And we need to be honest that the framers of the next game are going to have the same blind spots as the framers of the last one, unless we deliberately design against it.

The workers who were never invited to play Game A on fair terms — who had Game A's rules applied to them selectively, who were exploited by the DEAD game's Dominate and Extract moves without being offered the Adapt move's benefits — those workers have been designing alternatives for a long time. Not in think tanks. In mutual aid networks. In cooperative housing. In collective bargaining. In the commons-based organizing that Elinor Ostrom spent her career documenting.

[Robin D.G. Kelley's *Freedom Dreams*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/106895/freedom-dreams-by-robin-d-g-kelley/) documents the radical imagination that has persisted inside and alongside the DEAD game since the beginning — the visions of free, cooperative, dignified human organization that were being articulated by Black radicals in the nineteenth century, by labor organizers in the early twentieth, by the freedom movements of the mid-twentieth, consistently, through every wave of counter-revolution and co-optation. These are not new ideas waiting to be discovered by Game B thinkers. They are old ideas that were already there and were not invited into the room.

[Adrienne Maree Brown's *Emergent Strategy*](https://www.akpress.org/emergentstrategy.html) offers a framework for transformation that draws directly from this tradition — specifically from Octavia Butler's science fiction, from movement organizing, from somatics, from the logic of mycelial networks and murmuration. It is not a framework for people who are done with Game A. It is a framework built by and for people who Game A was done with first.

The framer problem is not solved by adding diversity to the Game B conversations, though that would help. It is solved by recognizing that the alternatives were already being built by the people who needed them most, and asking what it would mean to follow rather than design.

## The EVE Problem (Or: Why a Good Constitution Isn't Enough)

In 2003, the massively multiplayer game [EVE Online](https://www.eveonline.com) launched a fully player-driven economy in a science fiction universe. Players were given extensive freedom: to build corporations, set their own goals, make war or trade or exploration the organizing principle of their in-game lives.

Players also wrote constitutions. One of the most ambitious was for the Gallente Federation, a player organization that modeled itself on democratic ideals. They debated it. They ratified it. They were serious about it.

It lasted approximately eighteen months before replicating the same dynamics it was designed to prevent: concentrated power, informal hierarchies overriding formal governance structures, coordination failures, political factionalism that served internal games rather than collective purpose.

The constitution didn't fail because the players were bad people. It failed because Game A incentives operated inside Game B institutions, and the incentive structure won.

This happens. The Mondragon cooperatives — genuinely one of the most successful examples of large-scale cooperative organization in the twentieth century — have faced this pressure too: the pull toward conventional management structures when scale creates coordination problems that cooperative governance handles more slowly than hierarchy. The tension is real.

What this does not mean is that alternatives don't work. It means alternatives require not just different institutions but different habits of relation, different inner conditions, different cultural transmission of the values that make cooperation possible. You cannot write a constitution and call it done. You have to cultivate the soil that makes the constitution something people actually live by.

Game B institutions built by people still running Game A inner software will drift toward Game A outcomes. The missing piece is not better governance design. It is the inner work that makes a different way of relating possible. Which is why [A·3c](./05-A3c-soul-work.md) is not optional content.

## What the Betting Thread Was About

In 2021, in the early posts of this series' predecessor, I wrote about what was already being built at the margins of the collapsing formal economy. Platform cooperatives. Alternative currencies. Community land trusts. And I wrote about the migration of financial activity to Robinhood, to Coinbase, to Gemini — early crypto adoption as a form of lateral innovation around the formal financial system by people who the formal financial system had failed.

That analysis was contested at the time. It was interpreted as crypto enthusiasm, which it was not. It was an observation about behavior: when the formal system fails enough people badly enough, they build their own lateral infrastructure. The specific instruments they choose tell you about the failure mode they are trying to route around.

I call for a renewed sense of imagination, from work to government to science to spirituality — what are the next forms we should be betting on? Knowing that legalized betting is now a career path (Robinhood, Coinbase, Gemini), I am curious about the intersection of gaming as tools for social collaboration, ownership, and production in effort to reconstruct narratives around how societies operate.

The movement toward platform cooperativism in financial services, toward credit unions and community development financial institutions, toward decentralized finance that is actually controlled by its users — is real and growing. The instruments were imperfect. The direction was correct.

## Toward Game C (Work in Progress)

I don't have a name for what comes after Game B. Naming it too early would reproduce the framer problem — would invite the people currently most comfortable naming things to name the next thing before the people whose knowledge is most needed have been in the room long enough to influence the design.

What I can say: it is already being practiced in the margins. It looks like a mutual aid network in the Bronx after a climate emergency. It looks like a cooperative grocery in a food desert. It looks like an indigenous land back movement that is also a sophisticated experiment in commons governance. It looks like a gift economy practitioner in Richmond, Virginia, figuring out how to pay rent while practicing what they believe. It looks like a lot of things that don't have logos.

The practice precedes the theory. It always does. The theory's job is to make what is already working visible enough to spread. That is what I am trying to do with this series. I'm still figuring out if I'm doing it right.

What are you betting on?
